I'm into...

Horizon; out of the ashes.

It's my baby. If I'm ever fortunate enough to have an actual baby, Horizon will likely still be the one I'm embarassingly evangelical about.

Horizon: out of the ashes is a role playing game (or "RPG" if you're into that. I am.) played with a group of about 4 people, plus a narrator. The setting is Earth's only colony world of -wait for it- Horizon, themed in psuedo-realistic sci-fi flavor with a gritty texture and swirls of horror. The primary novelty lies in the concept of a widespread nanotechnology called IRIS that lives symbiotically in humans, enhancing just about everything about us, both physically and mentally. Through the many opportunities this offers, mankind could theoretically reach an idyllic state of existence, though the setting proposes a darker outcome, in which life on Horizon has been shaped by the failure of our maturity as a species to keep pace with our accelerating capabilities. This leaves age-old human shortcomings to destabilise our civilisation in what could have been a golden age of prosperity. What begins with oppression and defiance escalates by degrees until the world has been ravaged by violent insurrection and military fascism against a backdrop of betrayal and confusion. Eventually our entire way of life disintegrates in the throes of a plague, resulting in a brief period of total chaos. This gradually settles back into an uneasy balance of law and lawlessness however, faintly resembling the savagery and serenity of the American Old West. Except in space. With millions of microscopic robots making most people varying degrees of super-human. And overuse of their power erodes their sanity until they eventually become inhuman monsters. Or are they monsters?

These ideas began their convergence as a cohesive universe about 2012. I felt driven to make my own... something, and having finished university the previous year, I lacked any mundane outlet for the intensifying creative pressure. My first and most tantalizing dream was to make a video game, but I knew enough about the realities of that course to accept (thank heaven) that it was a pipe dream at best. On the other hand, I had dabbled in roleplaying throughout my time in tertiary education, enjoyed it, and was still in contact with enough people to make the playing of such a game a realistic ideal. This also offered a precious excuse to get together when we were all newly minted "adults" having just been ejected from the plush auspices of academia into a vast and counterintuitive "rest of your life" scenario. In the mean time, the creative itch had me scrabbling at the inside of my own skull, so in a drought of resources, but flooded by inspiration, I began one afternoon in a coffee shop and, for one reason or another, kept coming back to it. That's not to say that I knew what I wanted to make at the beginning. Ha! No.

Over years, Horizon is what emerged from a brew of isolated ideas I'd doodled in the margins or themes I'd played out in late nights when my mind was too busy for sleep. Some of them go far enough back that I was measuring my age in single digits. Over time though, once I decided I was going to do something with them, they had a way of rubbing off on each other, intertwining and growing together into something more homogeneous. Beginning to take on a new identity all their own. I still remember the first time I consciously turned down a suggestion because it "didnt fit". That came with the realisation that for it not to fit, there must be some identifiable trend for it to conflict with. Something Unique. A distinct vision and direction where once there had only been a nebulous cloud of possibilities. It was an exciting realisation, especially because it was unanticipated. As much time as I spent dreaming about Horizon, there was never a point when I simply decided something. I thought about it, wrote down my ideas for reference, then moved on, but in the background they seemed to develop with a life of their own. I would eventually return to write down something else, often months later, to find that what I had been cultivating recently was almost unrecognisable next to my last notes. In a sense, I've never really created anything. I was the soil and the gardner, the rain and the sun, but the seed that took root and the plant it became are a mystery, even to me.
